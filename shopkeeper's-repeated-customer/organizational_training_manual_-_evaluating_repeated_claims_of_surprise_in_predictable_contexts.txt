ORGANIZATIONAL TRAINING MANUAL
## **Evaluating Repeated Claims of Surprise in Predictable Contexts**

---

# **1. Introduction**

Repeated expressions of “surprise” in a stable, predictable environment can indicate many different underlying mechanisms: genuine perceptual shock, emotional discomfort, misunderstanding, rhetorical framing, strategic communication, or reverse justification.

This manual trains staff to:

- recognize these mechanisms,  
- collect evidence neutrally,  
- apply the diagnostic framework consistently,  
- classify behaviors accurately,  
- and recommend appropriate follow‑up actions.

This is a **diagnostic manual**, not a disciplinary one.

---

# **2. Core Concepts Staff Must Understand**

### **2.1 Expectation Updating**
People normally adjust expectations after repeated exposure.  
Failure to update may indicate:
- emotional conflict,  
- misunderstanding,  
- cognitive issues,  
- or strategic motives.

### **2.2 Surprise Categories**
Staff must distinguish among:
- **Genuine surprise**  
- **Emotional discomfort**  
- **Mistaken interpretation**  
- **Rhetorical surprise**  
- **Strategic surprise**  
- **Reverse justification**  

### **2.3 Neutrality**
Staff must not assume:
- deception,  
- incompetence,  
- bad faith,  
- or malicious intent.

All evaluations must be evidence‑based.

---

# **3. Roles and Responsibilities**

### **3.1 Observers**
Front‑line staff who witness the event.  
Responsibilities:
- capture real‑time behavior,  
- record timestamps,  
- note immediate verbal reactions,  
- document environmental context.

### **3.2 Reviewers**
Analysts who evaluate the collected data.  
Responsibilities:
- gather narratives,  
- perform incentive mapping,  
- run the timeline diagnostic algorithm,  
- apply the scoring rubric.

### **3.3 Supervisors**
Decision‑makers.  
Responsibilities:
- classify the behavior,  
- assign confidence levels,  
- recommend follow‑up actions,  
- ensure documentation completeness.

---

# **4. Training Modules**

## **Module 1 — Real‑Time Observation Skills**

### **Key Skills**
- Recognizing physiological surprise markers  
- Recording reaction latency  
- Capturing context neutrally  
- Avoiding interpretation during observation

### **Exercises**
- Watch short video clips and identify startle reflexes  
- Practice timestamp logging  
- Simulate predictable encounters and record reactions  

### **Performance Standard**
Observers must reliably distinguish:
- immediate surprise  
- delayed surprise  
- absence of surprise

---

## **Module 2 — Narrative Collection & Analysis**

### **Key Skills**
- Collecting statements without leading questions  
- Identifying narrative drift  
- Detecting escalation language  
- Comparing narratives across time

### **Exercises**
- Practice neutral questioning  
- Analyze sample narratives for inconsistencies  
- Identify rhetorical framing in provided transcripts  

### **Performance Standard**
Reviewers must detect:
- narrative insertion of surprise  
- post‑hoc reinterpretation  
- incentive‑aligned framing

---

## **Module 3 — Incentive Mapping**

### **Key Skills**
- Identifying organizational triggers  
- Recognizing when “surprise” benefits the claimant  
- Mapping procedural outcomes to claims

### **Exercises**
- Review organizational policies  
- Identify which statements trigger escalation  
- Analyze hypothetical cases for incentive alignment

### **Performance Standard**
Reviewers must determine whether surprise claims:
- shift responsibility,  
- justify intervention,  
- trigger emergency protocols,  
- or create documentation advantages.

---

## **Module 4 — Timeline Diagnostic Algorithm**

### **Key Skills**
- Comparing real‑time behavior to later narrative  
- Detecting temporal mismatches  
- Identifying behavioral incongruence  
- Running the full diagnostic sequence

### **Exercises**
- Work through sample timelines  
- Identify mismatches in provided cases  
- Classify behaviors using the algorithm

### **Performance Standard**
Reviewers must produce:
- a timeline congruence score,  
- a mismatch analysis,  
- and a preliminary classification.

---

## **Module 5 — Scoring Rubric Application**

### **Key Skills**
- Applying the 40‑point rubric consistently  
- Scoring emotional, cognitive, and strategic indicators  
- Computing the Reverse Justification Score (RJS)

### **Exercises**
- Score provided case studies  
- Compare scoring across trainees  
- Resolve scoring discrepancies

### **Performance Standard**
Reviewers must achieve:
- ≥90% scoring consistency across cases.

---

## **Module 6 — Decision‑Tree Classification**

### **Key Skills**
- Using the decision tree accurately  
- Distinguishing similar categories  
- Assigning confidence levels  
- Producing final classifications

### **Exercises**
- Classify sample cases  
- Resolve ambiguous scenarios  
- Practice confidence assignment

### **Performance Standard**
Supervisors must classify cases with:
- clear justification,  
- correct category selection,  
- and appropriate confidence levels.

---

# **5. Training Scenarios**

### **Scenario A — Genuine Surprise**
Predictable event becomes unpredictable due to context shift.

### **Scenario B — Emotional Surprise**
Customer labels fear as “surprise.”

### **Scenario C — Mistaken Interpretation**
Customer misunderstands the shopkeeper’s role.

### **Scenario D — Rhetorical Surprise**
Customer uses “surprise” to express objection.

### **Scenario E — Strategic Surprise**
Customer benefits from portraying encounters as unexpected.

### **Scenario F — Reverse Justification**
Customer inserts surprise into the narrative after acting.

Each scenario includes:
- observation logs,  
- narrative samples,  
- incentive maps,  
- scoring sheets,  
- classification keys.

---

# **6. Evaluation of Trainees**

### **6.1 Knowledge Tests**
Written tests on:
- surprise categories,  
- diagnostic logic,  
- incentive mapping.

### **6.2 Practical Tests**
Trainees must:
- observe staged encounters,  
- collect narratives,  
- run the diagnostic algorithm,  
- score the case,  
- classify the behavior.

### **6.3 Certification of Completion**
Supervisors confirm:
- competency in all modules,  
- consistent scoring,  
- accurate classification.

(Not a formal license; internal competency only.)

---

# **7. Ethical Standards**

Staff must:
- maintain neutrality,  
- avoid bias,  
- respect emotional states,  
- avoid coercive questioning,  
- preserve confidentiality,  
- document objectively.

This manual is for **diagnosis**, not punishment.

---

# **8. Maintenance & Updates**

The manual must be reviewed annually or after major incidents.  
Updates must incorporate:
- new behavioral research,  
- operational feedback,  
- policy changes.
