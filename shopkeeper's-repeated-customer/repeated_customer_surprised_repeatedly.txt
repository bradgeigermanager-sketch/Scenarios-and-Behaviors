Organizational SOP: Evaluation of Repeated Surprise Claims

## **1. Purpose**
This SOP establishes a standardized procedure for evaluating situations in which an individual repeatedly expresses “surprise,” “shock,” or “alarm” upon encountering a predictable person or event.  
The goal is to determine whether the behavior reflects:

- genuine perceptual surprise,  
- emotional discomfort,  
- mistaken interpretation,  
- rhetorical framing,  
- strategic communication, or  
- reverse justification.

This SOP ensures consistent, fair, and evidence‑based evaluation.

---

## **2. Scope**
This SOP applies to:

- retail environments,  
- customer‑facing operations,  
- compliance and oversight teams,  
- behavioral review units,  
- internal investigations,  
- supervisory personnel.

It covers **any repeated behavioral pattern** where “surprise” is expressed in a stable, predictable context.

---

## **3. Definitions**

### **3.1 Genuine Surprise**
A reaction consistent with immediate, involuntary physiological markers and aligned with the predictability of the event.

### **3.2 Emotional Surprise**
A reaction labeled as “surprise” but actually reflecting fear, discomfort, or aversion.

### **3.3 Mistaken Interpretation**
Surprise arising from misunderstanding roles, context, or expectations.

### **3.4 Rhetorical Surprise**
Use of “surprise” as a communicative device to express objection, disapproval, or concern.

### **3.5 Strategic Surprise**
Use of “surprise” to trigger policies, escalate situations, or justify actions.

### **3.6 Reverse Justification**
Post‑hoc insertion of “surprise” into a narrative to rationalize actions already taken.

---

## **4. Responsibilities**

### **4.1 Observers**
Responsible for real‑time data capture:
- timestamps,  
- behavioral markers,  
- immediate verbal reactions.

### **4.2 Reviewers**
Responsible for:
- narrative collection,  
- incentive mapping,  
- timeline comparison,  
- scoring rubric application.

### **4.3 Supervisors**
Responsible for:
- final classification,  
- confidence assessment,  
- recommendations,  
- documentation retention.

---

## **5. Procedure**

### **5.1 Phase 1 — Real‑Time Observation**
Observers must record:
- encounter time,  
- reaction time,  
- physiological markers (startle reflex, micro‑expressions),  
- contextual factors (location, predictability, presence of others),  
- immediate verbal statements.

All observations must be timestamped and stored in the event log.

---

### **5.2 Phase 2 — Narrative Collection**
Reviewers must collect:
- statements made immediately after the event,  
- statements made later the same day,  
- statements made in subsequent days,  
- statements made to different audiences.

Narratives must be compared for consistency, escalation, or retroactive reinterpretation.

---

### **5.3 Phase 3 — Incentive Mapping**
Reviewers must determine whether expressing surprise:
- triggers emergency protocols,  
- justifies intervention,  
- shifts responsibility,  
- creates documentation,  
- escalates complaints.

If incentives exist, note them explicitly.

---

### **5.4 Phase 4 — Timeline Diagnostic Algorithm**
Reviewers must compare:
- real‑time behavior,  
- immediate reactions,  
- post‑event narrative.

Identify:
- temporal mismatches,  
- behavioral mismatches,  
- narrative insertion,  
- counterfactual inconsistencies.

This determines whether surprise is genuine, emotional, mistaken, rhetorical, strategic, or reverse‑justified.

---

### **5.5 Phase 5 — Scoring Rubric Application**
Reviewers must apply the 40‑point rubric across:
- baseline cognitive updating,  
- domain‑specific updating,  
- emotional indicators,  
- rhetorical indicators,  
- mistaken interpretation indicators,  
- reverse justification indicators.

Compute:
- total score (0–40),  
- Reverse Justification Score (0–12).

---

### **5.6 Phase 6 — Decision‑Tree Classification**
Supervisors must classify the behavior using the decision tree:

- **Genuine Surprise**  
- **Emotional Surprise**  
- **Mistaken Interpretation**  
- **Strategic Surprise**  
- **Reverse Justification**  
- **Cognitive Disconnection**

Assign a confidence level:
- High (≥80%),  
- Moderate (50–79%),  
- Low (≤49%).

---

### **5.7 Phase 7 — Recommendations**
Based on classification, supervisors must recommend:

- role clarification,  
- emotional support,  
- communication coaching,  
- policy review,  
- environmental adjustments,  
- further cognitive evaluation,  
- or escalation to compliance or HR.

Recommendations must be documented.

---

## **6. Documentation Requirements**

All evaluations must include:
- event logs,  
- narrative samples,  
- scoring sheets,  
- timeline comparison results,  
- incentive mapping,  
- classification output,  
- recommendations.

Documents must be retained according to organizational policy.

---

## **7. Quality Assurance**

Supervisors must periodically audit:
- scoring consistency,  
- classification accuracy,  
- narrative capture quality,  
- observer neutrality,  
- documentation completeness.

Discrepancies must be corrected through retraining or procedural updates.

---

## **8. Ethical Considerations**

Evaluators must:
- avoid assumptions of bad faith,  
- avoid bias,  
- maintain confidentiality,  
- ensure fairness,  
- avoid coercive questioning,  
- respect emotional states.

This SOP is diagnostic, not punitive.

---

## **9. Revision and Maintenance**

This SOP must be reviewed annually or after any major incident involving repeated surprise claims.  
Updates must incorporate:
- new behavioral research,  
- operational feedback,  
- compliance requirements.
